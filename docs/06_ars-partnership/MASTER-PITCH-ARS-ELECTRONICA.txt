FESTIVALS HAVE VENUES. FESTIVALS DON'T HAVE VILLAGES.

A Partnership Proposal from Linz Hostel: The Social Impact Hub
to Ars Electronica


— I —

September. Linz.

A sound artist from Tokyo lands at 4 PM. A computational biologist from Lagos
checks in at 6. A 19-year-old from Graz who just won Create Your World carries
her suitcase through the door at 8.

By 10 PM, they're sitting in the same kitchen. Nobody planned this. The
building did.


— II —

1979. Ars Electronica is born. A festival in a city nobody associates with
technology. A bet that art and tech belong together. People thought it was
eccentric.

2009. Linz becomes European Capital of Culture. 3.5 million attendances. The
Ars Electronica Center expands. The Tabakfabrik begins its transformation. The
bet paid off.

2026. 122,000 people will come to Linz this September. They'll experience the
most ambitious programme Ars has ever mounted. Then they'll go sleep in
scattered hotels, eat breakfast alone, and miss every unscripted encounter the
festival architecture couldn't produce.

The stages exist. The galleries exist. The labs exist. The village doesn't.


— III —

You programme every minute of the festival. The panels, the exhibitions, the
performances, the workshops — all designed, all curated, all intentional.

But the most important moment of Ars Electronica? You don't programme it. You
don't even see it.

It's the conversation at breakfast between two people who would never have met
inside the programme. The midnight realisation in a shared kitchen. The lobby
encounter that becomes a three-year research collaboration.

Those moments are not happening. Because your visitors are scattered across
Linz in anonymous hotels, eating room service, separated by architecture
designed to keep strangers apart.


— IV —

Picture a breakfast table. Long, wooden, seats twelve.

At one end: a robotics PhD from ETH Zurich, in Linz for a Futurelab residency.
Next to her: the 17-year-old from Graz who has never left Austria. Across from
them: a festival volunteer from Brazil who is also a street artist. At the far
end: a Prix Ars jury member eating toast and reading his phone — until the
Brazilian mentions a project, and the jury member puts his phone down.

That table does not exist in any hotel in Linz. Hotels build tables for one,
tables for two, buffets where strangers avoid eye contact.


— V —

Now picture this instead:

A Prix Ars jury member finishes judging at 9 PM. Takes a taxi to a hotel 20
minutes away. Eats alone at the hotel bar. At breakfast the next morning, she
sits at a table for one. She will never know that the artist whose work she
awarded that night was staying three blocks away, eating breakfast alone at
exactly the same time.

Two realities. One signature apart.


— VI —

This is not a metaphor. It is architecture.

The sociologist Ray Oldenburg identified "third places" — spaces that are
neither home nor work — as the essential infrastructure for community
formation. Granovetter's research on weak ties proved that casual encounters
produce more novel ideas, more creative breakthroughs, more career-changing
connections than strong ties ever do.

Ars Electronica has exhibited these themes: GEMeinsam explored loneliness
through spatial design. Kyrielle won a 2025 Prix Ars Award of Distinction for
its meditation on human connection. Social Play examined urban isolation
through VR and AI.

You've given these ideas a stage. Now give them a building.


— VII —

The Basecamp.

90 beds, one kitchen, one community lounge, one address — walking distance
from your venues. Pod-style, design-led, built for the creative-technical
community you've spent 45 years cultivating. Priced for the cultural-
institution budgets they actually have: EUR 28/night instead of EUR 90+.

A permanent Ars Electronica installation in the lobby — curated by your
artists, rotating year-round. Not a September pop-up. Not a logo on a banner.
A space that is yours to programme inside a building that is ours to run.

The Flow Zone available as a satellite venue during the festival — screenings,
informal sessions, late-night conversations that would have nowhere else to
happen. Your residency artists housed. Your Futurelab visitors at home. Your
Festival University students and Create Your World participants finally
affordable to host.

Not for five days. For 365.


— VIII —

Where do Prix Ars Electronica winners sleep?

Not the famous ones — the emerging ones. The 24-year-old from Medellin who
just won an Award of Distinction and arrives in Linz with a stipend that
doesn't cover a hotel. The researcher from Dakar presenting at CAMPUS for the
first time. The three Festival University students from Seoul sharing a budget.

They sleep in a EUR 90+ hotel they can barely afford, 20 minutes from the
venues. They eat alone. They leave the festival at 10 PM because the last tram
is at 10:30.

Or they sleep in the village. Cook dinner with a Futurelab scientist. Meet a
jury member over coffee. Walk to the venue in five minutes. Stay up talking
until 2 AM because they're already home.

You decide which reality they get.


— IX —

Your mission: where art, technology, and society intersect.

Our building: a space designed around the loneliness crisis, using spatial
architecture to engineer human connection, serving the international creative-
technical community you've cultivated since 1979.

Those are not two projects. That is one project with two addresses.

If Ars Electronica doesn't partner with this, there is a gap to explain. Not
to us — to your own mission statement.


— X —

The numbers.

EUR 15,000 for a three-year founding partnership. That is 0.06% of your annual
revenue.

25 beds reserved during Festival Week at EUR 28/night — 70% below Linz hotel
rates. Transferable vouchers: bundle them into exhibitor packages and recoup
immediately.

A permanent installation space in the lobby, 12 months a year. Priority
booking for every residency guest, Futurelab visitor, and Festival University
student. A satellite event space during the festival at no additional cost.
SDG 3, 11, and 17 for your annual impact report. A tax-deductible
Betriebsausgabe for your accountant.

Full refund if we don't open. Every cent. Zero risk.

The question is not whether you can afford this. The question is what
institutional reason you'd give for not doing it.


— XI —

We are not asking for a decision today.

We are asking for a conversation. And if that conversation leads where we
believe it will, the next step is a one-page letter of intent. No money. No
legal complexity. No board approval required. Just a signed signal that Ars
Electronica believes the festival deserves a village.

That letter changes everything — not because of its financial value, but
because of what the name means. When Sparkasse sees "Ars Electronica" on the
commitment sheet, the conversation shifts from "who would stay at this hostel?"
to "how fast can we close?"

You are not doing us a favour. You are extending your own ecosystem into a new
space. First partner. Zero risk.


— XII —

The last exhibition closes. The stages go dark. 122,000 people start the
journey home.

The festival ends every September.

The village doesn't.


1979: you built the festival.
2027: build the village.
