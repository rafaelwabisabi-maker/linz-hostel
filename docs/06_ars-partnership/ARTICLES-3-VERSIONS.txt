TITLE: Festivals Have Venues. Why Don't They Have Villages?


Every September, 122,000 people fly to Linz, Austria for Ars Electronica —
the world's most important festival at the intersection of art, technology,
and society.

They experience five days of exhibitions, performances, panels, and workshops,
all designed to the minute. Then they go back to their hotels, eat dinner
alone, and miss every conversation that could have happened between sessions.

This is not an Ars Electronica problem. This is a structural problem with how
every major festival operates.

We programme stages. We curate galleries. We design workshops. But the spaces
between the sessions — the mornings, the late nights, the walks home — we
leave entirely to chance. And "chance," in practice, means scattered hotels
designed to keep strangers apart: closed doors, room service, private
bathrooms, tables for one.

The result: the most important moments of any festival — the accidental
encounters, the breakfast conversations, the midnight realisations — happen
far less often than they could. The architecture actively prevents them.


THE RESEARCH IS CLEAR.

In 1973, sociologist Mark Granovetter published "The Strength of Weak Ties" —
now one of the most cited papers in social science. His finding: casual
acquaintances (not close friends) are the primary source of novel information,
job opportunities, and creative breakthroughs. The person who changes your
career is not your best friend. It's the stranger you sat next to at breakfast.

Ray Oldenburg's concept of "third places" — spaces that are neither home nor
work — identified where these weak ties form: cafes, public squares,
barbershops. Places with low barriers to entry where people from different
backgrounds share physical space without a scheduled reason.

MIT's legendary Building 20 produced nine Nobel laureates, not because of its
research budget, but because of its architecture: shared hallways forced
physicists, linguists, and engineers to cross paths daily. Steve Jobs designed
Pixar's headquarters around the same principle — one central atrium, one set
of bathrooms, maximum collision.

The common thread: proximity + shared space + no agenda = the conditions for
unexpected connection.

Festivals have all the ingredients except the space. Thousands of people from
different disciplines, different countries, different perspectives — all in the
same city for the same week. The creative density is extraordinary. But it
disperses every evening into hotel rooms designed for isolation.


WHAT IF IT DIDN'T?

We're building what we call the Basecamp: a 90-bed community in Linz designed
specifically for the festival audience. Pod-style accommodation, a shared
kitchen, a community lounge we call the Flow Zone, and a permanent
installation space curated by cultural partners.

The design is intentional. The kitchen is communal because the architecture
gives you no private alternative. The lounge is positioned so that sitting
alone means sitting with everyone. The pod corridors are semi-open — you hear
someone's music through the wall and knock.

It is not a hostel in the backpacker sense. It is social infrastructure —
architecture optimised for the formation of weak ties within a creative-
technical community.

The model draws on proven precedents: magdas Hotel in Vienna (Austria's first
profitable social-mission hotel), Largo Residencias in Lisbon (creative-
neighbourhood symbiosis), The Social Hub in Amsterdam and Vienna (hybrid
accommodation maximising revenue per square metre through community design).


THE ECONOMICS WORK DIFFERENTLY THAN YOU'D EXPECT.

At EUR 28/night — compared to EUR 90+ at Linz mid-range hotels — the Basecamp
makes festivals accessible to the people who need them most: emerging artists,
international students, early-career researchers, and cultural workers on
institutional budgets.

For festival organisers, bed bundles at fixed rates solve the annual
accommodation scramble. Transferable vouchers can be bundled into exhibitor
packages. The logistics headache of coordinating six hotels across a city
disappears.

For the host city, a permanent community space that activates year-round — not
just during festival week — creates a node of cultural infrastructure that
justifies public investment and anchors a creative district.


THE BROADER QUESTION.

Post-COVID, cultural institutions face a structural challenge: how do you
rebuild the habit of in-person gathering? Attendance numbers are recovering —
Ars Electronica drew 112,000 in 2024 and 122,000 in 2025 — but the deeper
question is about quality of connection, not quantity of tickets.

The answer is not better programming. The programming is already extraordinary.
The answer is better architecture. Spaces designed so that the 122,000 people
who come for the festival actually meet each other.

Festivals have venues. It's time they had villages.

---

We're building the first one in Linz. If you're working on something similar
— or if you think your festival needs one — I'd like to hear from you.

TITLE: The most important conversation at Ars Electronica happens at
breakfast. Except it doesn't.


Last September, a Prix Ars jury member finished judging at 9 PM. She took a
taxi to her hotel — 20 minutes across Linz. Ate alone at the hotel bar. Went
to bed.

At breakfast the next morning, she sat at a table for one.

She never found out that the artist whose work she'd awarded that night was
staying in a hotel three blocks away. Eating breakfast alone. At exactly the
same time.

They would have had a lot to talk about.

---

This scene plays out at every major festival in the world. Thousands of people
from different disciplines, different countries, different perspectives — all
in the same city for the same week. The creative density is insane.

And every evening, it disperses. Into hotel rooms designed for one thing:
keeping strangers apart.

Closed doors. Room service. Elevators where nobody makes eye contact.
Breakfast buffets where you pick a table as far from everyone else as possible.

We accept this as normal. It's not normal. It's a design failure.

---

I've been working on a project in Linz called the Basecamp. It's a 90-bed
community space — pod-style, design-led, with a shared kitchen and a communal
lounge — built specifically for the people who come to this city for its
creative ecosystem.

The design is simple: make it architecturally impossible to be alone unless
you choose to be.

The kitchen is shared — because if there's no room service, you cook next to a
stranger. The lounge is positioned at the entrance — because if you have to
walk through a living room to get to your bed, you'll stop. The corridors are
semi-open — because hearing someone's music through the wall is an invitation
to knock.

None of this is revolutionary. It's what hostels have always done
accidentally. We're just doing it on purpose.

---

There's a sociologist named Mark Granovetter who proved in 1973 that your best
career opportunities, your most creative ideas, and your most life-changing
connections come from weak ties — casual acquaintances, not close friends.

The stranger at the breakfast table. The person in the kitchen at midnight.
The artist whose music you heard through the wall.

Hotels are designed to eliminate weak ties. The Basecamp is designed to
maximise them.

---

We're now in conversation with Ars Electronica — the festival that brings
122,000 people to Linz every September — about making the Basecamp their
permanent Festival Village.

Their artists at EUR 28/night instead of EUR 90+. Their jury members and
exhibitors under one roof. A permanent installation in the lobby, curated by
their artists, year-round.

The festival ends every September. The village doesn't.

---

If you've ever left a conference or festival thinking "I didn't meet the
people I was supposed to meet" — that wasn't your fault. It was the
architecture.

We're fixing the architecture.

TITLE: Hotels are loneliness machines. Here's what replaces them.


1/ Every hotel in the world is designed around the same principle: keep
strangers apart. Closed doors. Private bathrooms. Room service. Elevators
where nobody talks. This is not a bug. It's the product.

2/ For business travellers, that's fine. For festival-goers, researchers,
artists, and international students — the people who travel to CONNECT — it's
a disaster.

3/ 122,000 people attend Ars Electronica in Linz every September. They come
for the collisions between art, technology, and society. Then they go sleep in
hotels designed to prevent collisions between humans.

4/ The most cited paper in social science (Granovetter, 1973) proved that your
most important connections come from casual acquaintances — "weak ties." Not
your close friends. The stranger at the next table. Hotels eliminate weak ties
by design.

5/ MIT's Building 20 produced 9 Nobel laureates. Not because of its budget.
Because its hallways forced physicists, linguists, and engineers to cross
paths daily. Architecture creates connection. Or prevents it.

6/ We're building what we call the Basecamp in Linz. 90 beds. Shared kitchen.
Communal lounge. Pod-style rooms. One rule: the architecture makes it harder
to be alone than to be together.

7/ The kitchen is shared — no room service, you cook next to someone. The
lounge is at the entrance — you walk through a living room to get to your bed.
The corridors are semi-open — you hear someone's music and knock.

8/ Price: EUR 28/night. Linz hotels: EUR 90+. That's not charity — that's a
different business model. High density + community design + low staffing
overhead = viable at a price point that cultural-institution budgets can
actually pay.

9/ We're in conversation with Ars Electronica about making this their
permanent Festival Village. 25 beds reserved during festival week. A permanent
art installation in the lobby, curated by their artists year-round. Their
residency guests housed. Their students accommodated.

10/ The cost to them: EUR 15,000 for 3 years. That's 0.06% of their annual
revenue. Full refund if we don't open. The risk is literally zero.

11/ But this isn't about one festival or one city. Every major festival in the
world has the same problem: extraordinary programming, zero social
infrastructure. Stages without villages. Content without community.

12/ We think the Basecamp model is replicable. Linz is the proof of concept.
If it works here — adjacent to the world's most important art-tech festival —
it works anywhere.

Hotels are loneliness machines. Villages are connection machines. We're
building villages.
